First layer of courses


__D1\. Designing learning experiences__ — the basics of instructional design, regardless of audience or setting\. Diagnostic: design a 60\-minute learning experience from scratch and explain every choice\.

__D2\. Reading the learner__ — formative assessment, observation, and what evidence of learning actually looks like\. Diagnostic: watch three videos of student work and write a learner profile for each\.

__D3\. The learning sciences in 10 hours__ — a survey of what cognitive science, motivation research, and learning theory actually say\. Diagnostic: critique a popular education claim using research evidence\.

__D4\. Differentiation and equity__ — designing for diverse learners \(rural, multilingual, neurodivergent, varying prior knowledge\)\. Diagnostic: take one lesson and create three differentiated versions\.

__D5\. Feedback and assessment__ — how to give feedback that drives learning, and how to assess for understanding rather than recall\. Diagnostic: redesign one summative assessment as a learning\-oriented assessment\.

__D6\. The teacher as designer__ — your STEM background as an asset: how subject expertise translates into pedagogical content knowledge\. Diagnostic: take a hard concept from your professional field and design a way to teach it\.

__Second layer of courses__

____

Layer 2 is where the architecture starts to do real work\. The job changes fundamentally from Layer 1 to Layer 2: Layer 1 was about *taste\-testing*, Layer 2 is about *narrowing into intent*\. The learner now has signal across the four axes, and the architect's job is to produce a path that combines what attracted them with what they're capable of pursuing\.

Let me think about how to structure this before I list courses, because the "net" metaphor you used is exactly right and worth being precise about\.

Design principles for Layer 2:

A "net" means each Layer 2 course should be reachable from multiple Layer 1 courses, not just one\. This is what makes it a net rather than a tree\. A learner who took A1 \(Adolescents\) \+ B1 \(Project\-based\) \+ D6 \(Teacher as designer\) and a learner who took A6 \(Coaching teachers\) \+ B1 \+ C2 \(Curriculum at scale\) might both end up in a Layer 2 course on "Project\-based unit design for secondary STEM" — for different reasons, but coherently\.

This means Layer 2 courses are more specialized and combinatorial\. They sit at the intersection of two or three Layer 1 directions\. The architect's job is to find the intersections that match the learner's signature\.

Layer 2 courses should also be slightly longer or more demanding — same 10\-hour format, but now with more committed practice\. Layer 1 was discovery; Layer 2 is early commitment\. Each Layer 2 course should produce an artifact the learner could put in a portfolio\.

I'll organize the 40 courses into five Layer 2 tracks that emerge from Layer 1 patterns\. These aren't career destinations yet — they're "career neighborhoods" the learner is exploring\.

Why this catalog works as a net \(not a tree\):

A few things worth flagging:

Many courses live at intersections\. Course 2 \(Project\-based unit design\) belongs to Track 1 \(Practitioner\) but a learner who took A6 \+ B1 \+ C2 in Layer 1 might be routed to it as a stepping stone toward Track 2 or Track 4 work\. This is the net property\.

Tracks aren't mutually exclusive\. A typical learner takes 4\-6 Layer 2 courses, often pulling from 2 tracks\. Someone whose signal points to "instructional coach who designs PD experiences" might take 17, 18, 22 \(Track 3\) plus 9, 11 \(Track 2\)\. The architect should encourage this kind of cross\-track combination when the signature supports it\.

The artifacts compound into a portfolio\. By the end of Layer 2, a learner has 4\-6 substantive artifacts \(not certificates\) that demonstrate concrete capability in their emerging direction\. This is a fundamentally different value proposition than completion\-based learning\. It's also what makes graduation into Layer 3 \(certification\-grade work\) defensible\.

The Layer 1 → Layer 2 mapping is multi\-signal, not multi\-prerequisite\. The architect isn't checking "did they complete A1?" — it's reading "did A1 light up for them, and did B1 light up, and did D6 light up?" Pattern recognition across signals, not prerequisite gates\.

Cultural/contextual courses are baked in\. Notice 8, 21, 28, 38, 39 are explicitly LatAm\-relevant — that's intentional given HAI's market\. They also have a secondary function: they're courses no MIT or Stanford catalog will offer well, which is part of what justifies HAI as a regional institution rather than a generic global platform\.

What Layer 3 becomes: After Layer 2, the learner should know which 1\-2 tracks are theirs\. Layer 3 is then certification\-grade depth \(likely 4\-8 weeks per course, with practicum requirements\) within their chosen track, plus the explicit conversation: *"Based on what you've discovered, here's what kind of credential, degree, or practical pathway makes sense — and here's why\."*
